Case study: Utility SaaS

Redesigning Quote Creation Flow for a Utility CRM

Simplifying complex electricity & gas quoting workflows into a structured experience.

Role
UI/UX Designer
Industry
Utility (Electricity & Gas)
Scope
Quote Creation Flow
Tools
Figma

The Problem

In most utility CRM systems, quote creation is fragmented and unstructured. Sales teams are forced to jump between disparate tools and manual entry points.

Sales teams current struggles:
  • !Create quotes without structured customer data
  • !Use external tools to send quotes
  • !Create accounts after approval
  • !Manually link data across systems
⊟
Disconnected Quote Creation

Quotes are created separately without customer accounts

⚠
Incorrect Details

High risk of error in meter and consumption info.

◷
Onboarding Delays

Fragmented data slows down the final approval.

⇄
Manual Data Linking

Quotes and accounts are linked later, increasing errors

The Objective

01Combine customer, account, and quote creation
02Reduce manual entry steps significantly
03Support workflows with incomplete data scenarios
04Improve overall sales efficiency
Key insight

Quote creation wasn't complex, it was unstructured.

Sales agents were expected to complete a long, continuous form without clear segmentation or guidance. This created cognitive overload, increased the chances of errors, and made the quote creation process feel slower and more difficult than it actually was.

"The problem wasn't too many fields, it was lack of structure and decision guidance."

Design Pillars

01
Structured, Step-Based Flow

Breaking the quote process into clear steps allowed users to focus on one set of inputs at a time, instead of navigating a long, unstructured form.

02
Controlled Information Visibility

Only relevant fields are shown based on user inputs, reducing unnecessary complexity and keeping the interface focused.

03
Guided Progression

The flow provides clear direction on what to complete next, minimizing guesswork and reducing dependency on user memory.

04
Reduced Input Repetition

Related fields are grouped logically, and repeated inputs are minimized to avoid redundant effort during the process.

The Solution

A structured 4-step guided flow designed for speed and flexibility.

Step 01

Customer Information

Purpose

Capture basic customer details and determine account type (Residential vs Business) early on.

UX Decision

The system creates the customer account in the background as soon as Step 1 is validated. This eliminates the "Double Entry" problem found in legacy systems.

Step 02

Property & Meter

Purpose

Capture high-stakes utility data like MPAN/MPRN numbers and consumption patterns.

UX Decision

Inputs are grouped into logical "pods" based on fuel type. Inline validation checks against central utility databases in real-time to ensure accuracy.

Step 03

Payment (Optional)

Purpose

Collect bank details

UX Decision

Make optional to avoid friction and drop-offs. Sales teams often face friction when asking for bank details too early.

Step 04

Review & Share

Purpose

Compare and send quotes

UX Decision

Integrate sharing inside CRM. Instead of downloading PDFs, the CRM generates a unique secure link for the customer to review and sign their quote instantly.

Key Learnings

01

Structure reduces complexity

Even if the total number of fields remains the same, how you segment them determines perceived effort.

02

UX should guide, not just display

Internal tools shouldn't just be data containers; they should act as an assistant to the employee.

03

Internal tools deserve premium UI

Improving the aesthetic quality of enterprise tools leads to higher employee satisfaction and lower turnover.
